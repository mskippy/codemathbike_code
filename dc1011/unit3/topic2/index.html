3.2 Scriptwriting for Podcasts

Now that you’ve developed your podcast concept, it’s time to bring it to life through writing and sound. In this topic, you’ll learn how to write for the ear and how to design and conduct effective interviews. Work at your own pace through the sections below.

By the end of Topic 2, you will have a draft script outline for Episode 1 and (if your podcast includes interviews) a prepared question set.

Learning Goals

  • Write for the ear with clarity, rhythm, and a conversational tone.
  • Plan and conduct interviews with purpose and sensitivity.

Part 1: Scriptwriting Basics

Key Ideas: write like you talk (short, active sentences), include performance cues (pauses, emphasis), and structure your episode clearly: Intro → Main Story/Segments → Transitions → Outro.

Resources

  • Writing For the Ear (PDF)
  • Transom: Fostering the Art of Audio Storytelling
  • BBC Academy: How to Tell Stories Using Sound (transcript)

Part 2: Storytelling Formats & Techniques — Choose Your Track

Select the approach that fits your podcast. Work through the tips, do the quick practice, and use the links to explore examples.

Track 1 — Narrative Storytelling (Fiction or Non‑Fiction)

Learn: build tension with pacing and pauses; write vivid scenes; use dialogue, ambience, and SFX to "show, don't tell."

Practice: Write a 60‑second opening scene with narrator cues and at least two sound cues.

Starter Links:

  • BBC Academy — How to Tell Stories Using Sound
  • Radiolab — Episode Library (story craft examples)
  • Radiolab — “Tell Me a Story” (transcript)

Track 2 — Interview & Conversation

Learn: design open questions; listen actively; use ethical consent; handle shy or guarded guests.

Practice: Do a 3‑question mock interview. Record 30–60s and note one follow‑up you used.

Starter Links:

  • StoryCorps — Great Questions
  • Transom — The Art of the Pre‑Interview
  • Transom — Tips for Interviewing Shy People

Track 3 — Informative / Explainer / Review

Learn: structure information for listening; use plain language and examples; balance facts with personality.

Practice: Write a 45‑second explanation for your audience using one analogy and one real‑world example.

Starter Links:

  • Digital.gov — Plain Language Guide Series
  • Science Vs — Myth‑busting explainer (Spotify)
  • Pop Culture Happy Hour — Reviews & Commentary

Part 3: Drafting Your Script

Create a working outline for Episode 1. Keep it concise, readable, and ready for performance.

Your outline must include:

  1. Episode title & purpose (What story/idea are you exploring? Who is the audience?)
  2. Segment breakdown (Intro, Segments/Scenes, Transitions, Outro)
  3. Script excerpts for key moments (voice lines + SFX/music cues)
  4. Interview plan (if applicable): guest(s), 6–10 open questions, consent/care notes
