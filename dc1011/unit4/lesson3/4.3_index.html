Overview

Big idea: Thoughtful cinematography makes your story clearer, more engaging, and easier to edit. Good footage starts with planning, teamwork, and careful shot composition.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use core cinematography concepts such as camera angles, framing, rule of thirds, headroom, and lead room.
  • Apply basic lighting techniques using natural light and the idea of 3-point lighting.
  • Record clearer audio using lav mics or built-in mics in quiet environments.
  • Capture useful B-roll and establishing shots for your edit.
  • Film responsibly by respecting privacy, safety, and consent.
  • Begin filming and organizing your project footage in a clear folder structure.

You should have completed: your project pitch, script, storyboard, and shot list from the previous lessons in Unit 4.

Key Concepts – Cinematography Essentials

1. Framing & Composition

Use the rule of thirds, headroom, and lead room to make your shots look intentional, not random. Remember:

  • Rule of thirds: Place important subjects on the grid lines or intersections.
  • Headroom: Leave a bit of space above a person’s head—don’t crop them off.
  • Lead room: Leave space in front of where a person is looking or moving.
  • Angles: Wide (context), medium (actions), close-up (details/emotion), over-the-shoulder (instructions/conversations).

If you’re a visual learner, watch this quick breakdown of rule of thirds, headroom, and lead room before you start filming.

Watch: The Rule of Thirds, Head Room and Lead Room


2. Lighting Essentials

Good lighting makes your video easier to see and more professional. Start simple:

  • Face the light: Don’t put your subject in front of a bright window.
  • Key light: Main light on one side of the face.
  • Fill light: Softer light to reduce harsh shadows.
  • Back light: Light behind the subject to separate them from the background.
  • A single bright window + a room light can mimic 3-point lighting if you position carefully.

If your shots look dark or “muddy,” watch this demo to see how simple 3-point lighting can improve your video.

Watch: YouTube Lighting Setup for Beginners – 3 Point Lighting


3. Audio Basics

Viewers will forgive slightly messy video, but bad audio loses them fast. Aim for:

  • Quiet environment: Turn off music, fans, or open windows.
  • Get the mic close: Use a lav mic if you have one, or move the camera closer.
  • Do a test recording: Record 5–10 seconds and listen back before filming everything.
  • Use soft surfaces (curtains, clothing, carpet) to reduce echo.

If you’re not sure how to place or hide a lav mic, this short video shows you the basics.

Watch: How To Use a Lavalier Microphone the Right Way


4. B-Roll & Establishing Shots

A-roll = main talking parts. B-roll = extra footage that shows details, actions, and the environment. B-roll keeps your video interesting and helps you cover up cuts or mistakes.

  • Establishing shots: Wide shot of the room/kitchen/workspace at the start.
  • Detail shots: Hands mixing, tools, ingredients, close-ups of important steps.
  • Movement: Slow pans, tilts, or push-ins to add energy.
  • Use B-roll to match what you are talking about in the voiceover or A-roll.

If you’re not sure what to film for B-roll, watch these easy examples and steal a few ideas.

Watch: 7 Easy & Cinematic B-Roll Shots for YouTube Vlogs (using a phone)


5. Responsible Filming – Privacy & Consent

When you film, you’re not just making a video—you’re also handling other people’s privacy. In our school projects:

  • Ask permission before filming anyone who might be recognizable.
  • Avoid filming random students in the background if you can.
  • Follow school rules about where you are allowed to film.
  • Don’t post classmates online without their consent (and parent/guardian permission for minors if required).

Before you film in hallways or public spaces, pause and think about who might end up on camera and whether you have their permission.

Activity A – Project-Based Camera Lab: Shot Composition Challenge

In this camera lab, you are not filming random test shots. You will practice real shots from your own video project so that what you film today can be used in your final edit.

Step 1 – Choose a Short Sequence from Your Video

With your group, pick one short part of your video (about 10–30 seconds of finished video time). For example:

  • Explaining the ingredients you’ll need
  • Showing a key step in the process (e.g., mixing, assembling, or cutting)
  • Introducing the finished product or result

Look at your script, storyboard, and shot list to decide which moment to focus on.


Step 2 – Film Multiple Versions of the Same Shots

For your chosen sequence, each person should film multiple versions of the same shots, using the cinematography ideas from this lesson.

Each student films at least:

  • 1 Establishing shot of the location/workspace (try at least 2 angles – wide from different positions)
  • 1 Medium shot of the main action (try 2 versions – different angles or slightly different framing)
  • 1 Close-up shot of an important detail (try 2 versions – different angles or distances)
  • 1 B-roll / creative shot (e.g., slow pan, over-the-shoulder, low-angle, or movement shot)

While you film, pay attention to rule of thirds, headroom, lead room, lighting, and audio.


Step 3 – Compare Shots and Choose the Best Ones

Now it’s time to compare and get feedback.

  • If you are in a group: Sit together and watch each person’s clips for the same shot type (e.g., everyone’s establishing shots). For each shot type, decide:
    • Which version looks the most professional and why?
    • Which framing feels clearest for the audience?
    • Does the shot use good lighting and avoid distractions?
  • If you are working mostly on your own: Ask 1–2 classmates or your teacher to quickly watch your clips and answer:
    • Which version would you choose for the final video?
    • What could I change to improve the framing or lighting?

Step 4 – Save Your Best Clips in a Camera Lab Folder

After you get feedback, choose your strongest clips and save them in a clearly named folder.

  • Create a folder called CameraLab_FirstName<> inside your project folder.
  • Rename your favourite clips clearly, for example:
    • Establishing_Desk_01.mp4<>
    • Medium_Mixing_02.mp4<>
    • CloseUp_Ingredients_01.mp4<>
    • Broll_PourIntoPan_01.mp4<>
  • Optional: keep extra versions in a folder called Extras<> if you might still use them.

By the end of Activity A: you should have several usable, well-composed clips that fit your actual video project, not just practice shots.

Activity B – Begin Filming Your Project Footage

Now that you’ve practised your shots in the Camera Lab, it’s time to start filming real footage for your video. The goal of this activity is to capture your main A-roll (talking/explaining) and start building a library of strong B-roll (supporting visuals).

Step 1 – Set Up Your Project Media Folder

Create a folder for this project so your footage doesn’t get lost on your device.

  • Create a main folder named: Unit4_Footage_FirstName<>
  • Inside it, create subfolders:
    • Aroll<> (your main speaking/instruction clips)
    • Broll<> (detail shots, process shots, environment)
    • Audio<> (only if you record sound separately)
    • Extras<> (any test clips you might still use)

Track A – Individual filmmakers: this folder holds all of your own clips.
Track B – Group filmmakers: each person still has their own folder, even if you filmed together.

Step 2 – Film Your A-Roll (Main Talking Parts)

Use your script and storyboard to film the main parts where you explain, demonstrate, or talk directly to the audience.

  • Film in a quiet location with good lighting.
  • Use the framing rules from this lesson (rule of thirds, headroom, lead room).
  • Record more than one take of important lines or steps.
  • After each short section, pause and check that:
    • Your voice is clear and loud enough.
    • Nothing important is cut off in the frame.
    • There are no major distractions in the background.
  • Save each clip in your Aroll<> folder with clear names, e.g. Aroll_Intro_01.mp4<>, Aroll_Step2_02.mp4<>.

If you’re in a group: rotate roles (camera operator, presenter, audio check) so everyone gets experience and support.

Step 3 – Film Supporting B-Roll

Now capture the visual details that will make your video more interesting and easier to edit.

  • Use your shot list to guide which B-roll you need.
  • Film close-ups of hands, tools, ingredients, or important steps.
  • Grab one or two establishing shots of your workspace or environment.
  • Include at least one movement shot (slow pan, tilt, or push-in).
  • Let the camera roll a little longer than you think you need (2–3 seconds before and after the action).
  • Save these in your Broll<> folder with clear names, e.g. Broll_HandsMixing_01.mp4<>, Broll_FinishedProduct_01.mp4<>.

Tip: If someone is helping you film, remind them what you want the audience to notice in each shot (e.g., “focus on the texture of the dough”).

Step 4 – Daily Wrap-Up: Review & Fill the Gaps

Before you finish for the day, take 5–10 minutes to review what you filmed.

  • Quickly skim through your Aroll<> and Broll<> folders.
  • Check for:
    • Clips that are too dark, too noisy, or badly framed.
    • Important steps or moments that are missing.
  • Highlight or mark missing shots on your shot list.
  • Make a short note for next class: “Next time we need to re-film…”

Optional but smart: back up your footage to OneDrive, Teams, or a USB drive so you don’t lose your work if something happens to your device.

By the end of Activity B: you should have a mix of A-roll and B-roll saved in your project folder, ready to be organized and edited later.

Filming Checkpoint – Before You Start Production

Before moving on to editing (production), you must confirm that you have enough good-quality footage to build a strong video. Editing only works when you have lots of options — different angles, different takes, and enough B-roll to cover transitions and mistakes.

Why This Matters

Professional filmmakers always film everything first before they even open their editing software. Editing is much easier when you have:

  • Multiple takes of the same moment
  • Different angles to choose from
  • Extra B-roll to make your video engaging
  • Clean audio for every section of your script

If you start editing with not enough footage, you’ll waste time, get frustrated, and have to go back and re-film. This checkpoint prevents that.

What You Must Have Before Production

Use this checklist to make sure you’re ready to start editing.

  • A-roll: All main talking and demonstration clips filmed, with at least 2 usable takes of important moments.
  • B-roll: Enough detail shots, close-ups, process shots, and establishing shots to cover your entire script.
  • Clear audio: No loud background noise, wind, or echo on important lines.
  • Multiple angles: At least 2–3 different angles for key steps so your edit doesn’t look static.
  • Organized folders: A-roll and B-roll saved neatly into your project folder with clear filenames.
  • Shot list check: You’ve marked which shots are done and which ones still need to be filmed.
  • Backup: Your footage is backed up to OneDrive/Teams/a USB device.

Quick Self-Assessment

Answer these before moving on:

  • Do I have more than one take for important scenes?
  • Do I have B-roll for every major step or moment?
  • Would someone who hasn’t seen my script understand the video from my footage?
  • Could I begin editing today without needing to re-film?
